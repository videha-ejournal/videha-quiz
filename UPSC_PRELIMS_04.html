Q52. What does an empty seat represent in early Buddhist iconography? (2026)
Explanation: Source: UPSC GS Paper I 2026, Q2. In early Buddhist art—dating back to the pre-Christian era at sites like Sanchi Stupa and Bharhut—artists did not depict the Buddha in human form. Instead, they utilized aniconic (symbolic) markers to indicate his presence. The empty seat (or empty throne) typically symbolizes the Buddha's enlightenment under the Bodhi tree and his ongoing state of meditation. Hence option (a) is the correct answer. Other key symbols of this era include: The Wheel (Dharma Chakra): The Buddha's First Sermon at Sarnath. The Bodhi Tree: The moment of Enlightenment. The Stupa: The Mahaparinibbana (the Buddha's passing away).

Q57. Among the four main forms of existence of life recognized in Jainism, which one of the following is NOT included? (2026)
Explanation: Source: UPSC GS Paper I 2026, Q15. In Jain philosophy, rebirth is classified into four principal forms of existence (Gati) determined by an individual’s karma. These four Gatis are: Deva (heavenly beings) Manushya (human beings) Tiryancha (animals, birds, insects, and plants) Naraka (infernal beings) Option (a) is correct. Devas, or heavenly beings, constitute one of the four recognised forms of existence in Jainism. Although they enjoy celestial pleasures as a result of virtuous karmas, they continue to remain within the cycle of birth and rebirth. Option (b) is incorrect. Yakshas are regarded as semi-divine attendant deities in Jain tradition. However, they are not included among the four principal Gatis recognised in Jain philosophy. Option (c) is correct. Manushya, or human beings, form one of the four Gatis and are considered the most capable of attaining spiritual progress and ultimately liberation (Moksha). Option (d) is correct. Tiryancha refers to sub-human forms of life, including animals, birds, insects, and plants, and is recognised as one of the four principal forms of existence in Jainism.
Q58. The Hallisalasya painting in the Bagh Caves represents: (2026)
Explanation: Source: UPSC GS Paper I 2026, Q16. The Hallisalasya painting of the Bagh Caves is particularly celebrated for its vibrant portrayal of festive folk dance scenes, reflecting the lively cultural traditions of ancient India. Situated in present-day Madhya Pradesh along the banks of the Baghini River, the Bagh Caves date to the Gupta period and are among the finest examples of early Indian rock-cut architecture and mural art. The caves are especially renowned for their exquisite fresco-style paintings, which display remarkable skill in colour composition, human expression, and narrative depiction. Influenced by the artistic tradition of Ajanta, the murals portray themes ranging from courtly life and music to dance, religious motifs, and everyday social activities. The Hallisalasya scene stands out for its dynamic movement, emotional richness, and detailed representation of performers, offering valuable insight into the artistic and cultural life of the Gupta age, often regarded as the “Golden Age” of Indian art and culture.
Q59. Which one of the following statements with regard to the Moidams, built by the Tai-Ahom kingdom and inscribed as a World Heritage Site by UNESCO, is/are correct? 1. They acted as army fortresses. 2. They were recreation centres of the Royals and Nobles. 3. They were burial grounds of the Royals and Nobles. 4. They were battle drill centres of the Royals and Nobles. Select the answer using the code given below: (2026)
Explanation: Source: UPSC GS Paper I 2026, Q27. Statement 1 is incorrect. Moidams were not constructed as military fortresses or defensive structures. Their primary purpose was funerary and ceremonial rather than strategic or military. Statement 2 is incorrect. The Moidams were not recreation centres for the Ahom royals and nobles. Instead, they were sacred burial mounds associated with royal funerary practices and ancestor worship. Statement 3 is correct. Moidams served as the burial grounds of Tai- Ahom kings, queens, and nobles. These mound-like structures contained underground vaulted chambers where the deceased were interred along with personal belongings, royal objects, and occasionally attendants or animals as part of elaborate burial rituals. Statement 4 is incorrect. There is no historical evidence to suggest that the Moidams functioned as battle drill or military training centres for the Ahom aristocracy. The Moidams of Charaideo in Assam reflect the unique funerary traditions and Tai cosmological beliefs of the Ahom dynasty. Recognising their outstanding cultural significance, UNESCO inscribed them as India’s 43rd World Heritage Site in 2024.
